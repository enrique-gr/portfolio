Aerospace & Flight

I’ve always looked at aircraft and launch vehicles the same way I look at complex engineering systems: reduce them down to fundamentals, understand the constraints, and study why each design choice exists. These platforms stand out because they reward deep curiosity and disciplined analysis—they are the products of engineering done at the limits.

SR-71 Blackbird Recon · Mach 3+

Outrun the threat instead of dodging it.

The SR-71 is a perfect example of engineering pushed into the extreme. I’m drawn to how its design handles continuous heating, material expansion, and mission reliability at Mach 3+, where every decision has direct consequences for survivability and performance.

F-22 Raptor 5th Gen Fighter

Stealth, supercruise, and control laws.

The F-22 has always stood out to me for how seamlessly aerodynamics, avionics, and control laws work together. It’s a system where real-time software, stealth geometry, and thrust vectoring feel integrated rather than layered on top of each other.

YF-23 “Black Widow II” Prototype

The stealthy “what if” path.

I’ve always been fascinated by the YF-23 because it represents a completely different path the ATF program could have taken. Its emphasis on signature reduction, high-speed stability, and a unique planform shows how different engineering priorities can shape an entirely separate solution to the same problem.

Saturn V Moon Rocket

Brute force with precision.

The Saturn V is engineering at full scale—staging decisions, clustered engines, and a mission profile that demanded reliability under enormous forces. I appreciate how every part of the vehicle reflects disciplined systems engineering under extreme constraints.

F-16 “Viper” FBW Pioneer

Relaxed stability as a feature.

The F-16’s intentionally unstable design has always been one of my favorite examples of control theory in action. It shows how software, real-time feedback, and control surface authority can turn instability into agility.

Favorite Real-World Missions

These missions shaped how I think about engineering under pressure: making decisions with incomplete data, building systems that tolerate failure, and staying calm when the margin for error is effectively zero. They’re real examples of control, discipline, and teamwork at scale.

Apollo 13

Real-time systems engineering: reconfiguring hardware, procedures, and checklists on the fly to bring a damaged spacecraft home safely.

Falcon 9 First Landing

Closed-loop control, iteration, and telemetry-driven decision-making—turning something that looked impossible into a repeatable, reliable system.

Perseverance & Ingenuity

Precision robotics and control in an environment you can't touch—every gram, watt, and line of code has to earn its place.

Counter-UAS Field Exercise

Sensor fusion, autonomy, and fast decision loops—treating the airspace as a dynamic system that has to be understood, not just observed.

Training & Movement

Training has always felt similar to engineering work for me—set a clear target, define the constraints, and apply consistent effort. I’m drawn to simple movements performed with high intent, where progress comes from discipline rather than tricks.

Calisthenics

Weighted pull-ups & dips

Weighted pull-ups and dips have become my primary strength movements. I focus on controlled execution, progressive overload, and keeping the work honest—no shortcuts, no wasted reps.

Style Heavy, clean, controlled
Why Simple movements, high intent

Strength Training

No reps left in reserve

Most of my working sets are done at a high effort level—usually 0–1 RIR. It feels similar to tuning a control loop: push hard, observe the response, adjust, and build resilience over time. The structure and discipline carry over directly into engineering work.

Philosophy Make top sets memorable
Carryover Same mindset as debugging & EKF tuning
